Company Profile


The Urban Indigenous Theatre Company is a non-profit company dedicated to the empowerment and health of the Aboriginal community through Theatre and Film. Primarily youth centric, we provide workshops, projects and programs intended to bring Indigenous youth and community members an expanded worldview, self-motivation, hard and soft skills in creative thinking, product development / creation, production, finishing / marketing as well as general personal health practices.

We offer Training, Development, Production and Mentorship services for youth, professionals and educators interested in working with Aboriginal people and content. UITC creates employment for Aboriginal Artists and craftspeople through our productions and programs, and as of the posting of this document are the only Aboriginal controlled Theatre company in the province of Manitoba.

Product / Service / Licensing

Service Name:
Aboriginal Arts Training and Mentorship Program
The Aboriginal Arts Training and Mentorship Program is a program designed to engage Aboriginal and At-risk non-Aboriginal youth in after school activities and training related to the production of Theatre and Film based works. Intended as a community program to engage youth in Arts activities for general health and well being, the program has a direct impact on suicidal, criminal, and other negative behaviours.

This is an annual program requiring substantial input for it's activities. Activities include the classes in acting for stage, acting for screen, directing for stage, directing for screen, writing for stage, writing for the screen, theatre production, video production and editing, product packaging and marketing. Included are on-going Mentorships with active Aboriginal Theatre and Film Artists.

In addition to the provision of training for the youth the AATMP program specifically targets boundaries to participation and it's budget accurately reflects the cost of food security and transportation for the youth through daily meals and bus tickets to and from the program.

This program is designed for youth between the ages of 12 and 19.

Class capacity 16
Classes 4 per week
Minimum length 40 weeks (80 hours / youth).

Service Name:
New Legends Program
The UITC New Legends Program is an Arts based youth development program intended to assist young people in the creation of a personal voice. Through Stage, Media, Music and Cultrally based Arts the NLP gives youth the opportunity to create self-defined work for disbursement to the general public both locally and on-line.

Youth are provided a UITC managed budget to create, produce and package their works.

UITC retains no rights to youth works, save those of use for advertising purposes. Program length is tailored to the availability of funds.

Session capacity - 15 youth.
Minimum length, - 40 total hours.

Service Name:
Empowerment through Drama and Cultural Arts - In-School Program
Empowerment through Drama and Cultural Arts is a program designed specifically for implementation in schools during school hours. Offering both Theatre and Media based development activities the UITC EDCA program engages students in the creation of a class work in order to expose youth to Aboriginal culture and provide a bridge between traditional and contemporary arts.

Program Length is determined by contributing schools.

Minimum length - 20 weeks (40 in class hours).

Service Name:
Empowerment through Drama and Cultural Arts - After School Program
The Empowerment through Drama and Cutural Arts - After School Program is designed specifically for use by schools and communities in engaging youth in Theatre and Film based Arts activities using the school as the primary location. In addition to the development of youth soft and hard skills relating to the creation, development, production, finishing and marketing of Arts based works, this program assists in creating a direct "safe-zone" bond between students and their schools.

Program length is determined by participating schools.

2 sessions per week
Minimum length 20 weeks (80 total hours)
Session capacity 16 youth

Service Name:
Theatre Arts Workshops
UITC Theatre Arts Workshops vary in length and are designed to bring Aboriginal youth a sense of Theatre as an extension of Traditional Storytelling, allowing them to bridge the divide between different cultural forms. Available workshops include Puppeteering, Mask, Mime, Physical Theatre, Scripted Theatre, Improvistion and Clown.

Minimum Length - 1 Day (4 hours)
Workshop Capacity - 16 Participants / day

Service Name:
Film Arts Workshops
UITC Film Arts Workshops are intended to give youth a bridge between the cultural differences separating traditional storytelling and contemporary film/video. Led by Industry professionals these workshops create short films as collaborations between youth and active film and video artists. Neither UITC nor the professional Artists involved retain any rights to the finished films save those of use in advertising and/or online/offline portoflio presentation. Finished videos are distributed to national film festivals as entries by the youth.

Minimum Length - 7 Days (6 hours per day)
Workshop Capacity - 10 youth / workshop

Strategic alliances:

We are working to build an infrastructure for the Aboriginal Theatre and Film Arts markets in Manitoba, through the development of young people as future producers of cultural and arts based products. This section of industry is under-represented in the world market and offers the opportunity for strong intervention into Aboriginal social issues in addition to increased employment among Aboriginal people.

Additionally, UITC is dedicated to the effective collection of information regarding it's programs over the next 3 years. This will allow the company to create manuals and trainers sessions for the disbursement of our program models to social agencies, Theatre organizations and reservation / rural community groups in Canada and around the World.

Sector information

Unique applications:

Our Training modules are the only aboriginal controlled theatre programs that offer to bridge Aboriginal Storytelling with contemporary forms as a tool for youth and community intervention in this region. Having recognized that the art is inseparable from the issues actively facing a community we have found that our programs function best (re: 7 Oaks school Division EDCA progam feedback) when utilizing cultural techniques (drum singing, ceremony, traditional dance) in addition to Theatre / Film skills development.

Key / Major clients:

The Youth Peacebuilding Project
7 Oaks School Division
Resource Assistance for Youth
Fisher River First Nation
The Graffitti Gallery
Broadway Neighborhood Center

Success stories:

The Artistic director of UITC, Columpa Bobb is designer of the original Aboriginal Arts Training and Mentorship Program Project Partnership from 2004 until 2013. Our General Manager and Program Director were involved in the program's delivery from 2010 until 2013. During it's life the "Ab Arts" project delivered daily classes to Aboriginal youth and training in all aspects of Theatre and Film. From the program have come many graduates who are actively working in the fields of Education, Theatre, Film, Social Services, the Culinary Arts, Mining and other industries. The program provided over 13,000 individual participant hours of training, a Main stage production, several short films as well as hundreds of hours of Mentorship annually. One graduate of the program describes it as having saved her life as she considered suicide in her early teens and another became the 2010 American Indian Film Festival Winner for Best Actor thanks to a film he took part in with Argyle High School. The program was closed by it's host organization in June 2013 and control of the program has reverted to the artists involved.

In 2013, our partnership with the Youth Peacebuilding project resulted in the creation of a show entitled The Moving Gallery 2.0 in which Aboriginal youth and Newcomer youth worked together to present a production in the Winnipeg Fringe Festival. Utilizing Spoken word, Visual Art and Media the youth delivered a commentary on the connective issues they all face after researching the Canadian residential School system. The commonality they found included feelings of displacement, loss, culture shock and hopelessness. The poetry, monologues and performance pieces they wrote in response to these feelings and the bonds they forged formed the bulk of the show. The production garnered two 4 of 5 star reviews in major local papers and was well received by the viewing public.

In 2013, UITC provided workshops to Fisher River First Nation in Theatre skills as well as the Ontario Native Welfare Administrators Association for community forum theatre as a development / intervention tool.
