Company Profile

2. ORGANIZATION OF EASTERN CARIBBEAN STATES
SEWERAGE AND SEWAGE TREATMENT STUDY

Morrison Hershfield Limited was contracted by the World Bank and
the Organization of Eastern Caribbean States (OECS) to
complete a pre-feasibility study of proposed and potential
sewerage and sewage treatment projects in the OECS countries
of St. Kitts & Nevis, Antigua & Barbuda, Dominica, St. Lucia,
St. Vincent & the Grenadines and Grenada. The objective was
to identify priority projects in each of the countries and to
develop a framework by which international lending agencies
can assess the feasibility of financing these projects. The
final report included an outline of what each country has to
do in order to implement particular projects.

The project scope included the following aspects:

- Technical Aspects - determination of appropriate
technologies for collection, treatment and disposal
of sewage. This includes preliminary layout of collection
systems, comparison of treatment methods, and determination
of appropriate disposal options including land based
solutions and submarine ocean outfalls. The investigation
included an assessment required for each project and a
cost/benefit analysis of the alternative presented.

- Institutional/Legislative - in order to properly implement
the proposed technical solutions, each country's capacity
to operate, maintain and regulate sewage facilities was
evaluated. This included an analysis of designated
management and technical personnel in the National Water and
Sewage Authority, organization structure, management
information systems, current cost recovery measures,
applicable legislation and regulations, linkages with
regulatory and planning authorities etc.

- Socio/Economic - included in the implementation strategy for
identified priority projects is the people's ability and
willingness to pay. In the context of developing countries,
household income and preferences govern whether a community
wants and can afford to implement any type of a sewage
system.

The project commenced in January, 1996 and the final report
was submitted in December 1996.

2. TOWN OF MILLET
WATER SUPPLY AND DISTRIBUTION STUDY

The Town of Millet, with a population of 2,000, uses a
water distribution network supplied by a central reservoir
which in turn is supplied by ground water wells. A barely
sufficient water supply capacity and low system pressures
warranted an overall Water Supply and Distribution Study.

Data was collected and reviewed to assess the existing water
supply capacity. Future Town populations were then projected
to identify future water supply needs. The distribution
system was modelled utilizing the "WATER" computer program
and later calibrated using flow and system pressure data
collected in the field. The calibrated model was used to
identify system modifications required to boost system
pressures now and in the future and to simulate future
expansion of the distribution system. Recommendations for
phased construction of system and supply modifications were
submitted in report form.

Subsequent to submission of our report, the Town commissioned
Maxim to retain a groundwater hydrologist to identify and
complete a suitable additional well water supply and
authorized Maxim to connect the wells to the existing
reservoir. The new wells also generated upgrading of water
treatment plant controls and the installation of a new
distribution pump. Maxim provided design plans and
specifications, received construction tenders, reviewed and
recommended for contract award and provided construction
inspection and contract administration.

Client: Town of Millet, Alberta

Year: 1995

4. CAMP WAINWRIGHT (DEPARTMENT OF NATIONAL DEFENCE)
WATER PRODUCTION SYSTEM AUTOMATION STUDY

Maxim was commissioned to study the potable water production
facilities located on Camp Wainwright to determine how to
automate the system to enable production hours to be expanded
from 12 to 24 hours while reducing required operating hours
from 12 to eight.

The existing system includes a river intake pumping station
which pumps raw water to a large raw water storage reservoir
(lake). A conventional 1.5 MGD water treatment plant treats
raw water from the lake using clarification and filtration,
and then pumps the potable water to an underground storage
reservoir. Booster pumps deliver the water, through metering
stations which monitor the flows, from the underground storage
to elevated storage reservoirs at the Town of Wainwright and
at Camp Central.

The water treatment plant has a programmable logic controller
(PLC) in which the automatic mode has not reliably operated
since installation. A telemetry system uses several terminal
units (RTU's) to link all the facilities to the water
treatment plant, except for the river pumping station which
is independently operated. The RTU's do not operate
reliably.

Maxim personnel visited the site several times to collect
data, receive input from system operating personnel and
camp engineers, and share ideas. As a result, Maxim personnel
gained a comprehensive understanding of the system and the
client's goals. From this, a simple clear report was written
which identified the required changes to the existing control
and telemetry system, and to areas of the process in order to
automate the plant.

Changes to the control system included upgrading and

4. CAMP WAINWRIGHT
WATER PRODUCTION SYSTEM AUTOMATION STUDY

Maxim was commissioned to study the potable water production
facilities located on Camp Wainwright to determine how to
automate the system to enable production hours to be expanded
from 12 to 24 hours while reducing required operating hours
from 12 to eight.

The existing system includes a river intake pumping station
which pumps raw water to a large raw water storage reservoir
(lake). A conventional 1.5 MGD water treatment plant treats
raw water from the lake using clarification and filtration,
and then pumps the potable water to an underground storage
reservoir. Booster pumps deliver the water, through metering
stations which monitor the flows, from the underground storage
to elevated storage reservoirs at the Town of Wainwright and
at Camp Central.

The water treatment plant had a programmable logic controller
(PLC) in which the automatic mode had not reliably operated
since installation. A telemetry system used several terminal
units (RTU's) to link all the facilities to the water
treatment plant, except for the river pumping station which
is independently operated. The RTU's did not operate
reliably.

Maxim personnel visited the site several times to collect
data, receive input from system operating personnel and
camp engineers, and share ideas. As a result, Maxim personnel
gained a comprehensive understanding of the system and the
client's goals. From this, a simple clear report was written
which identified the required changes to the existing control
and telemetry system, and to areas of the process in order to
automate the plant.

Changes to the control system included upgrading and
re-programming the PLC to provide automatic operation,
installing a user friendly computerized Man Machine Interface
(MMI) to assist operators to control, track and manage the
system, installing new reliable RTU's to relay information
from all the remote facilities over existing telephone lines
to the PLC and the MMI. Changes to the process included
installing new pumps, valves, and instrumentation to support
24 hour operation.

The report was well received and implementation of the
recommendations was completed.

4. CAMP WAINWRIGHT
WATER PRODUCTION SYSTEM AUTOMATION STUDY

Maxim was commissioned to study the potable water production
facilities located on Camp Wainwright to determine how to
automate the system to enable production hours to be expanded
from 12 to 24 hours while reducing required operating hours
from 12 to eight.

The existing system includes a river intake pumping station
which pumps raw water to a large raw water storage reservoir
(lake). A conventional 1.5 MGD water treatment plant treats
raw water from the lake using clarification and filtration,
and then pumps the potable water to an underground storage
reservoir. Booster pumps deliver the water, through metering
stations which monitor the flows, from the underground storage
to elevated storage reservoirs at the Town of Wainwright and
at Camp Central.

The water treatment plant had a programmable logic controller
(PLC) in which the automatic mode had not reliably operated
since installation. A telemetry system used several terminal
units (RTU's) to link all the facilities to the water
treatment plant, except for the river pumping station which
is independently operated. The RTU's did not operate
reliably.

Maxim personnel visited the site several times to collect
data, receive input from system operating personnel and
camp engineers, and share ideas. As a result, Maxim personnel
gained a comprehensive understanding of the system and the
client's goals. From this, a simple clear report was written
which identified the required changes to the existing control
and telemetry system, and to areas of the process in order to
automate the plant.

Changes to the control system included upgrading and
re-programming the PLC to provide automatic operation,
installing a user friendly computerized Man Machine Interface
(MMI) to assist operators to control, track and manage the
system, installing new reliable RTU's to relay information
from all the remote facilities over existing telephone lines
to the PLC and the MMI. Changes to the process included
installing new pumps, valves, and instrumentation to support
24 hour operation.

The report was well received and implementation of the
recommendations was completed.

raw water from the lake using clarification and filtration,
and then pumps the potable water to an underground storage
reservoir. Booster pumps deliver the water, through metering
stations which monitor the flows, from the underground storage
to elevated storage reservoirs at the Town of Wainwright and
at Camp Central.

The water treatment plant had a programmable logic controller
(PLC) in which the automatic mode was not reliably operated
since installation. A telemetry system used several terminal
units (RTU's) to link all the facilities to the water
treatment plant, except for the river pumping station which
is independently operated. The RTU's did not operate
reliably.

Maxim personnel visited the site several times to collect
data, receive input from system operating personnel and
camp engineers, and share ideas. As a result, Maxim personnel
gained a comprehensive understanding of the system and the
client's goals. From this, a simple clear report was written
which identified the required changes to the existing control
and telemetry system, and to areas of the process in order to
automate the plant.

Changes to the control system included upgrading and
re-programming the PLC to provide automatic operation,
installing a user friendly computerized Man Machine Interface
(MMI) to assist operators to control, track and manage the
system, installing new reliable RTU's to relay information
from all the remote facilities over existing telephone lines
to the PLC and the MMI. Changes to the process included
installing new pumps, valves, and instrumentation to support
24 hour operation.

The report was and implemented and the construction is
complete.
